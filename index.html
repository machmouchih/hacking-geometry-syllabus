Course Overview

The "Hacking" Philosophy

This studio explores the intersection of geometric rigor and architectural reality. You will not just create shapes; you will define construction systems. Starting with a specific geometric taxonomy on Day 1, you will physically test its structural limits, translate its logic into a digital tool, and deploy it onto a challenging site to create a functional Pavilion.

1. Experiments

Instead of generic workshops, your physical testing is now dictated by your Day 1 Selection. You will join one of these "Material Families" to stress-test your concept:

View Material Families & Tests ‚ñº

Group A: The Developable Surface

Task: Folding & Buckling Tests. Where does the crease fail?

Materials: Cardboard, scoring tools, cutting mats.

Group B: The Tensile Surface

Task: Stretching & Anchoring Tests. Measuring tension limits.

Materials: Piano wire, pliers, soap solution.

Group C: The Solid Volume

Task: Carving & Stacking Tests. Understanding mass and voids.

Materials: Plaster of Paris blocks, modeling clay.

2. Concept & Taxonomy

Don't just research shapes; research failures. Select your taxonomy from the menu below and produce a "Logic Diagram" of its construction rules.

View The Concept Menu (Day 1 Selection) ‚ñº
  • Minimal Surfaces (e.g., Frei Otto)
  • Developable Surfaces (e.g., Frank Gehry)
  • Platonic Solids & Geodesic Spheres (e.g., Fuller)
  • Translational Surfaces & Vaults (e.g., Saarinen)
  • Ruled Surfaces & HP Surfaces (e.g., Candela)
  • Freeform Curves & NURBS (e.g., Zaha Hadid)
  • Freeform & Subdivision Surfaces (e.g., Peter Cook)
  • Pipe Surfaces & Knots (e.g., Piano & Rogers)
  • Space-Filling Polyhedra (e.g., Jean Renaudie)
  • Constant Mean Curvature (CMC) (e.g., Grimshaw)

3. 3D Modeling

Translate your physical findings into a digital system (Rhino/Maya). Then, apply one of these Exploration Types to adapt your geometry to a hostile Site Antagonist.

View Exploration Types ‚ñº
  • Apply Striation & Pattern
  • Deconstruct into a Skeleton
  • Introduce Layering & Stratification
  • Carve Sub-Volumes
  • Aggregate as Clustered Parts
  • Define the Boundary Condition
  • Develop Spliced or Interlocked Joints
  • Incorporate Pleating
  • Differentiate the Intensity
  • Create a Nested Loop

Detailed Weekly Schedule

Week 1 (Jan 26): The Draft (Day 1 Selection).

In-Class: "Geometric Menu" Intro.
Action: You pick your geometry family today. No generic exploration.

Week 2 (Feb 02): Targeted Experiments.

Physical experiments specific to your geometry (e.g., soap film for tensile). Models must be measured (grams/cm).

Week 3 (Feb 09): Real World Failures.

Lecture: How geometry fails in construction. Build a complex physical iteration and break it.
Deliverable: The Autopsy Report.

Week 4 (Feb 16): Strategic Taxonomy (Presidents Day).

Asynchronous: The Logic Diagram. Create a flowchart of your geometry's rules. This is required before opening software.

Week 5 (Feb 23): Digital Translation.

Live Demo: Rhino vs. Maya Showdown. Translate the Week 4 Logic Diagram into a clean digital Base System.

Week 6 (Mar 02): The Site Antagonist.

Select a site that challenges your geometry. Establish "Site Rules."

Week 7 (Mar 09): MIDTERM REVIEW.

Presentation: The Geometric System + The Physical Proof + The Site Strategy.

Week 8 (Mar 16): NO CLASS — SPRING BREAK
Week 9 (Mar 23): Program & Narrative.

Define the User Journey. Moving from "Object" to "Place." (Post-Break Reset).

Week 10 (Mar 30): Rule-Based Layout.

Constraint: Design Plan/Section using *only* your geometric rules.

Week 11 (Apr 06): Construction Logic.

Deep Dive: Tectonics & Joints. Produce a "Wall Section."

Week 12 (Apr 13): Visualization.
